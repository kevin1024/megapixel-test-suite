MegaPixel Test SuiteChapter 1: Getting Started

Starting the application

Double-click MegaPixel.app in the Workspace File Viewer.
Or
Drag its icon into the application dock, then click it.

MegaPixel Test Suite draws the display-calibration patterns of the 240p Test Suite natively on a NeXT screen. When it starts, it opens a single window showing the first pattern — the SMPTE color bars — and nothing else. There are no inspectors, palettes, or documents to manage; every pattern is drawn on demand and the whole application is driven from the menu and the keyboard.

The window's title always names the pattern you are looking at, so you never have to guess what is on screen.

Choosing a pattern

Press the Right arrow key for the next pattern, or Left arrow for the previous one.
Press Up or Down to move through a pattern's variants.
Or
Choose Patterns from the main menu and pick a pattern by name.
For a list of every pattern and what it is for, see “The Patterns” in the reference at the end of this guide.

The suite contains twelve patterns. The arrow keys are the quickest way to move among them: Left and Right step through the patterns in order, and Up and Down cycle the variants of the pattern you are on — the cell size of the checkerboard, the number of gray steps, or the level of the color bars.

Not every pattern has variants. When one does, the window title and the loading screen show which variant is active — for example, SMPTE Color Bars (100%) or Checkerboard at its one-pixel setting — so the current state is always visible even when the difference on screen is subtle.

The Patterns menu lists the same twelve patterns by name if you would rather point than step. The View menu duplicates Next, Previous, and Variant for the same reason.

MegaPixel Test SuiteChapter 2: Calibrating Your Display

Setting white balance with the color bars

Select SMPTE Color Bars (it is the first pattern).
Press Up to switch between the 75% and 100% bars.
Adjust your monitor's color controls until the gray bars look neutral, with no color tint.

The color bars are seven vertical bars — white, yellow, cyan, green, magenta, red, and blue — above a strip of reference blocks at 100%, 0%, 50%, and 75% gray. Use them to judge color purity and white balance: on a correctly set-up display the white and gray bars carry no color cast, and the colored bars are clean and fully saturated.

The 75% bars are the broadcast-standard levels and are the ones to trust for most adjustments. The 100% bars drive every lit channel to full and are useful for checking that the display is not clipping or blooming at peak white. The active level is always shown in the window title, so you can tell the two apart even though they look similar.

Setting black level with PLUGE

Select PLUGE / Black Level.
Lower brightness until the darkest bar just disappears into the background.
Raise brightness until the two brighter near-black bars are just barely visible.

PLUGE — Picture Line-Up Generation Equipment — places three bars just above black next to a 100% white reference. It sets the display's black level (brightness): when it is right, true black stays black while detail just above black is still visible.

Press Command-I to raise the whole field slightly, which makes the near-black steps easier to separate on displays with crushed shadows.

Caution: Set black level in a dimly lit room. Ambient light washes out the near-black bars and leads you to raise brightness too far, lifting black into gray.

Checking geometry and convergence

Select Convergence Grid to check alignment and linearity.
Select Circles / Geometry to check for stretching.

The Convergence Grid draws a regular grid of white lines with a bright border and a centre crosshair. The lines should be straight, evenly spaced, and square to the edges of the screen; on a color CRT the red, green, and blue should sit exactly on top of one another with no colored fringes.

The Circles / Geometry pattern draws concentric circles with more circles in the corners. The circles should be round, not egg-shaped, and the same size in the centre and the corners — if they are not, the display's geometry needs adjustment.

Testing resolution at native size

Press Command-F to fill the whole display.
Select Vertical Stripes or Horizontal Stripes, then press Up to reach the 1-pixel setting.
Press Escape to return to the window.
For the complete list of variants for each pattern, see “The Patterns” in the reference.

For a true pixel-for-pixel test, use full-screen mode so that nothing scales the pattern between the drawing and the glass. The 1-pixel stripes and the 1-pixel checkerboard are the maximum-frequency resolution tests the display can be asked to show.

If those single-pixel patterns render as crisp, distinct lines, the display is resolving full detail. If they blur into flat gray, that is the display or the cabling reaching its limit — not the pattern, which is drawn exactly one device pixel wide.
